Learning from new information

  • People are flooded with new information from multiple sources: internet, tv, own and others’ experiences, …

  • This seems like a good thing, right?

  • Well, not necessarily. It depends on how information is processed and interpreted.

  • We are going to see that:

    • Interpretation of information is often influenced by biases.
    • These biases affect decision-making and may have important social consequences.

Bayesian updating

  • Economic theory often assumes that agents respond to new information by updating their beliefs according to Bayes’ rule.

Bayes’ rule

\[ Prob \left( A \mid B \right) = \frac{Prob \left( B \mid A \right) \times Prob \left( A \right)}{Prob \left( B \right)} \]

\(A\) and \(B\) are events.

\(Prob \left( A \right)\) and \(Prob \left( B \right)\) are the probabilities of observing \(A\) and \(B\).

\(Prob \left( A \mid B \right)\) and \(Prob \left( B \mid A \right)\) are the conditional probabilities of observing \(A\) given \(B\) and observing \(B\) given \(A\), respectively. That is, \(Prob \left( A \mid B \right)\) is the probability of \(A\) occurring given that \(B\) has occurred, while \(Prob \left( B \mid A \right)\) is the probability of \(B\) occurring given that \(A\) has occurred.

Bayesian updating: example 1 (cont’d)

  • Question: Given the new information you have received, what is the probability that I am 36 or older?

  • Was you answer zero?

  • If yes, then congrats! You have just updated your beliefs according to Bayes’ rule.

  • Now let’s do the math.

Bayesian updating: example 1 (cont’d)

  • \(A =\) I am 36 or older.
  • \(B =\) I was born after the Netherlands won UEFA Euro 1988.
  • Probability that I am 36 or older given no other information: \[ Prob \left( A \right) = p \in \left[ 0, 1 \right] \]
  • Probability that I was born after the Netherlands won UEFA Euro 1988: \[ Prob \left( B \right) = 1 \]

Bayesian updating: example 1 (cont’d)

  • Probability that I was born after the Netherlands won UEFA Euro 1988 if I am 36 or older: \[ Prob \left( B \mid A \right) = 0 \]
  • By Bayes’ rule, the probability that I am 36 or older conditional on being born after the Netherlands won UEFA Euro 1988 is: \[ Prob \left( A \mid B \right) = \frac{Prob \left( B \mid A \right) \times Prob \left( A \right)}{Prob \left( B \right)} = \frac{0 \times p}{1} = 0 \]

Bayesian updating: example 2

  • John wants to buy a new car. The choice is between two models, named Sporty and Comfy.

  • John does not know which model will give him the greatest satisfaction. He therefore gathers information by searching the internet, asking his friends, and so on.

Bayesian updating: example 2 (cont’d)

  • Any new information John receives is called a signal.

  • For example, if he reads a consumer review that recommends buying Sporty, then we say that he receives a “Sporty signal”.

  • Suppose that John has received a Sporty signal. How should he update his beliefs?
  • Let \(p\) and \(1-p\) denote, respectively, the prior probabilities that John puts on Sporty and Comfy being better for him: \[ Prob \left( \text{Sporty is better} \right) = p \quad Prob \left( \text{Comfy is better} \right) = 1 - p\]

Bayesian updating: example 2 (cont’d)

  • Let \(\theta\) denote the probability that John puts on the signal being precise: \[ \begin{multlined} Prob \left( \text{Sporty signal} \mid \text{Sporty is better} \right) \\ = Prob \left( \text{Comfy signal} \mid \text{Comfy is better} \right) = \theta \end{multlined} \]

    The parameter \(\theta\) captures things such as the trust John places in others’ opinions.

  • Similarly, \(1 - \theta\) is the probability that John puts on the signal being imprecise: \[ \begin{multlined} Prob \left( \text{Sporty signal} \mid \text{Comfy is better} \right) \\ = Prob \left( \text{Comfy signal} \mid \text{Sporty is better} \right) = 1 - \theta \end{multlined} \]

Bayesian updating: example 2 (cont’d)

  • Overall probability of receiving a Sporty signal:

    \[ \begin{split} Prob \left( \text{Sporty signal} \right) & = Prob \left( \text{signal precise} \right) \times Prob \left( \text{Sporty is better} \right) \\ & \phantom{aaa} + Prob \left( \text{signal imprecise} \right) \times Prob \left( \text{Comfy is better} \right) \\ & = \theta p + \left( 1 - \theta \right) \left(1 - p \right) \end{split} \]

  • Thus, by Bayes’ rule we obtain:

    \[ \begin{multlined} Prob \left( \text{Sporty is better} \mid \text{ Sporty signal} \right) \\[4pt] \begin{aligned} \phantom{aa} & = \frac{Prob \left( \text{Sporty signal} \mid \text{Sporty is better} \right) \times Prob \left( \text{Sporty is better} \right)}{Prob \left( \text{Sporty signal} \right)} \\[1pt] \phantom{aa} & = \frac{\theta p}{\theta p + \left( 1 - \theta \right) \left(1 - p \right)} \end{aligned} \end{multlined} \]

Bayesian updating: example 2 (cont’d)

  • Suppose that:
    • The prior probability that John puts on Sporty being better is \(p = 0.4\)
    • The probability that he puts on the signal being precise is \(\theta = 0.9\)
  • Thus, if John receives a Sporty signal, the posterior probability he should put on Sporty being better is: \[ \begin{split} Prob \left( \text{Sporty is better} \mid \text{Sporty signal} \right)& = \frac{0.4 \times 0.9}{0.4 \times 0.9 + 0.6 \times 0.1} \\[4pt] & = \frac{0.36}{0.42} \approx 0.86 \end{split} \]

The problem with bayesian updating

  • Bayesian updating is what John should do if he wants decisions to be as rational as possible.

  • However, using Bayes’ rule is not always easy.

    • It can involve cumbersome calculations.
    • It requires assigning specific numbers to all variables involved in the updating process (in our example, \(p\) and \(\theta\)).

  • It is therefore not surprising that people commonly suffer from biases in how they interpret new information.

  • We are going to focus on two of such biases, known as confirmation bias and law of small numbers

Confirmation bias

Confirmation bias

The tendency of individuals to interpret new information in a way that confirms or supports their initial beliefs and opinions.

  • This is particularly true when information is ambiguous.

Confirmation bias: example

  • Imagine that John reads the following: “Sporty manufacturers did their best to create a car with low fuel consumption and that is enjoyable to drive”.
  • Have the Sporty manufacturers succeeded? We don’t know.

  • If John believes that Sporty is the best choice for him, then he may interpret this information as “Sporty manufacturers created a car with low fuel consumption and that is enjoyable to drive”.

  • This bias implies that John may be overconfident when making his choice.

Confirmation bias: experimental evidence

  • Darley and Gross (1983) asked participants in an experiment to evaluate the academic ability of a 9-year-old child.

Read the paper HERE

Confirmation bias: experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • Half of the participants in each priming group (low-income vs. middle-class) were shown a video of the child answering questions. The other half were not shown this video.

  • The video was constructed to be inconsistent and uninformative about the child’s abilities:

    • She answered some questions correctly and some incorrectly.
    • She appeared to be fairly attentive on some portions of the video, and unresponsive and distracted on other portions.

Confirmation bias: experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • Participants were then asked to evaluate the child’s academic abilities.

Confirmation bias: experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • Participants in the low-income group rated the child’s ability to be lower than participants in the middle-class group.

  • How participants interpreted the same video depended on initial information about the child:

    • The video increased participants’ perceptions of ability if the child was believed to be from a middle-class background.
    • Conversely, it decreased participants’ perceptions of ability if the child was believed to be from a low-income background.

Confirmation bias: experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • Intuition
    • Participants formed a belief about the child’s abilities on the basis of assumptions about the relationship between socio-economic status and academic ability.
    • They then interpreted what they saw in the video so as to make it consistent with their belief.

Confirmation bias: more experimental evidence

  • Lord, Ross, and Lepper (1979) presented participants in an experiment with two fictitious studies about death penalty and its effectiveness as a deterrent to murder.

Read the paper HERE

Confirmation bias: more experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • Participants read:

    1. A study that confirmed their initial beliefs about capital punishment.
    2. A study that contradicted these initial beliefs.

  • Each subject was therefore presented with both prodeterrence and antideterrence information (in random order).

  • Overall, the evidence was intended to be inconclusive.

Confirmation bias: more experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • Finally, participants were then asked questions about:
    1. Their attitudes towards death penalty.
    2. How well or poorly the study had been conducted and how convincing it seemed as evidence on the deterrent efficacy of death penalty.

Confirmation bias: more experimental evidence (cont’d)

Example of prodeterrence information

Kroner and Phillips (1977) compared murder rates for the year before and the year after adoption of capital punishment in 14 states. In 11 of the 14 states, murder rates were lower after adoption of the death penalty. This research supports the deterrent effect of the death penalty. […]

Example of antideterrence information

Palmer and Crandall (1977) compared murder rates in 10 pairs of neighboring states with different capital punishment laws. In 8 of the 10 pairs, murder rates were higher in the state with capital punishment. This research opposes the deterrent effect of the death penalty. […]

Confirmation bias: more experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • Participants had a tendency to rate positively evidence that was consistent with their initial opinion, and rate negatively evidence inconsistent with their opinion.
    • Proponents of capital punishment regarded the prodeterrence study as significantly more convincing and better conducted that the antideterrence study.
    • Conversely, opponents of capital punishment regarded the prodeterrence study as significantly less convincing and less well conducted than the antideterrence study.

Confirmation bias: more experimental evidence (cont’d)

  • The consequence of exposing the two groups to an identical body of inconclusive empirical evidence was an increase in polarization of attitudes.
    • When asked for their final attitudes relative to the beginning of the experiment, proponents (opponents) reported that they were more (less) in favour of death penalty.

Law of small numbers

Law of small numbers

The mistaken belief that small samples are representative of the population they are drawn from.

  • Imagine that John searches for new information by asking strangers on the street about their opinion on Sporty and Comfy.

  • Suppose that the first 4 respondents all give John a “Comfy signal”.

Law of small numbers (cont’d)

  • A sample of 4 respondents is way too small to be representative of the population!

  • Because of the law of small numbers, John may overinterpret this as due to differences in quality rather than chance.

  • Moreover, he may expect that the next signal will also be supportive of Comfy.

Gabler’s fallacy and hot hand bias

  • There are two sides to the law of small numbers that we need to consider.

Gabler’s fallacy

The mistaken belief that if a random event occurred more frequently than normal during the past, then it is less likely to happen in the future (or vice versa).

It can occur when the probability of each outcome is known.

Hot hand bias

The overly optimistic belief that someone who has been successful in a task or activity is likely to be successful again in further attempts.

It can occur when there is uncertainty over the probability of each outcome.

Gambler’s fallacy

  • You are sitting at a roulette table and must choose which colour to bet on. The last 6 roulette spins gave the result below:

Red Red Red Red Red Red

  • Which colour would you bet on?

  • In 1913, at the Monte Carlo casino, the roulette ball stopped on Black 26 times in a row, causing gamblers to lose huge sums of money betting against what they thought to be a hot streak that could not possibly be sustained.

  • However, roulette spins are independent events: the past has no effect on what will happen in the future.

Gabler’s fallacy (cont’d)

  • We know that each spin has a 50% chance of being Red and 50% chance of being Black (for simplicity, let’s ignore the Zero ).

  • Should we expect about 50% of spins to be Black?

    • Only if the number of spins is very large.
    • Conversely, if the wheel is spun a relatively small number of times, we may well observe a streak of the same result.
  • Here is where the bias comes into play. As noted by Tversky and Kahneman (1971), people expect that the essential characteristics of a random process are reflected in every segment of the process, even if small.

Gabler’s fallacy (cont’d)

  • Put differently: in considering four spins of a roulette wheel, people regard the sequence

    Black Red Black Red

    to be far more likely than the sequence

    Red Red Red Red

  • Chance is often viewed as a self-correcting process: after observing a long streak of Red , most people erroneously believe that Black is now due, presumably because the occurrence of Black will result in a more representative sequence than the occurrence of an additional Red .

Hot hand bias

  • The term derives from the saying that athletes have hot hands when they repeatedly score, causing people to believe that they are on a streak and have a greater chance of success in future shot attempts.
  • Often, this belief is biased in an optimistic direction.

Hot hand bias: experimental evidence

  • Gilovich, Vallone, and Tversky (1985) examined the shooting records of the Philadelphia 76ers during the 1980-81 season.

  • They also assessed players’ and basketball fans’ beliefs about sequential dependence between consecutive shots.

Filter bubbles and misinformation

  • The algorithms used by search engines, social medias, and marketers to personalize advertising and user-directed content create filter bubbles that limit exposure to differing viewpoints.

Filter bubble

A state of intellectual isolation that may result from algorithms feeding individuals with information that supports what they already believe and like, based on their past click-behaviour and search history.

  • This allows misinformation campaigners to exploit human biases by tailoring messages to people who are already inclined to believe them.

Filter bubbles and misinformation (cont’d)

  • Example: John is considering whether or not to vaccinate his children against Covid-19. He fears that vaccines may contain harmful ingredients or weaken children’s immune system.

One day John comes across this Facebook post.

Filter bubbles and misinformation (cont’d)

  • The post is (purposely?) ambiguous: are children hospitalized because of the vaccine or despite it? What is the sample size?

  • Confirmation bias may cause John to interpret the post as evidence that vaccines are harmful. He may then give a like to the post without checking whether the news is fake or not.

  • Facebook’s algorithm will automatically show John more content shared by the post’s creator, and will suggest similar sponsored content in John’s feed.

Filter bubbles and misinformation (cont’d)

  • Due to the law of small numbers, John may come to regard a relatively small sample of such posts as highly persuasive.

  • He may therefore become overconfident that the evidence against vaccines is overwhelming, when it is not.

  • Levy (2021) conducted an experiment to formally test the hypothesis that Facebook’s algorithm increases polarization by disproportionately exposing people to news matching their own opinions.

Facts vs. opinions

  • Over the last two decades, opinion programs have come to dominate television.

Facts vs. opinions (cont’d)

  • Straight news programs (profess to) impartially report “just the facts”.
  • Opinion programs convey the anchor’s and speakers’ perspective on the news of the day.
  • Different opinion programs present distinct, and often conflicting, narratives about reality.
  • If viewers trust the statements made in opinion programs just as they would those made on straight news, failing to distinguish between opinion and fact, then diverging narratives across programs can lead different segments of the population to hold dramatically different views of reality.

Opinions as facts

  • Bursztyn et al. (2023) conducted an experiment with a sample of regular viewers of the two most popular cable news networks: MSNBC and Fox News.

  • Subjects were asked to provide their best guess about an objective statistic relating either to:

    1. A general fact relating to the US economy
    2. The unemployment rate in the US
    3. GDP growth in the US
    4. Median weekly earnings in the US
    5. The number of COVID-19 cases

Read the paper HERE

Opinions as facts (cont’d)

  • Guesses were made in relation to a specific, randomly selected date in the last few years.

  • To inform their guess, respondents could choose one of four TV clips, which were all excerpted from shows broadcast on the same week as the date pertinent to their guess.

  • Incentive to learn objective facts: subjects were informed that if their guess was within 5% of the true value, they would win an Amazon gift card.

    • The value of the gift card was randomized: half of the respondents were offered a $10 card and half a $100 card.

  • The results are relatively similar across all domains and reveal a substantial preference for opinion programs: roughly 75% of Fox News viewers and 60% of MSNBC viewers chose one of the two opinion shows.

Opinions as facts (cont’d)

  • People’s tendency to rely on opinion programs as sources of information has important consequences.

  • Bursztyn et al. (2023) examined the effect of diverging narratives between opinion programs in a high-stakes setting: the early stages of the COVID-19 pandemic in the US.

  • Result: viewers of different opinion programs significantly differ in their timing of adoption of preventative behaviour.

Opinions as facts (cont’d)

  • Both Fox News’ Hannity and Tucker Carlson Tonight have a right-wing slant.

  • However, Tucker Carlson Tonight emphasized the seriousness of the COVID threat as early as January 2020, while Hannity long ignored or downplayed it.

  • Bursztyn et al. (2023) surveyed a sample of Fox News viewers and found that…

    • Viewers of Tucker Carlson Tonight changed behaviour in response to the virus earlier than other Fox News viewers.
    • Viewers of Hannity changed behaviour later.
